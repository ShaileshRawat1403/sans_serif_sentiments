## 🔤 A–C

**AI Hallucination**
When an AI confidently invents incorrect or fictional information. Not just a mistake — but a believable lie.

**AI Governance**
Policies, people, tools, and workflows that ensure AI is used ethically, legally, and safely across a team or organization.

**API (Application Programming Interface)**
A structured way for different software systems to talk to each other. AI tools often connect to apps or websites through APIs.

**Bias (in AI)**
When AI outputs favor or discriminate unfairly, often due to biased training data or skewed assumptions.

**Chat Completion**
A full response generated by an AI model after a prompt — includes formatting, intent understanding, and context continuation.

**Conversational UX**
UX (user experience) design focused on natural conversation. Think AI that doesn’t just respond — it listens, clarifies, and adapts.

**Content Drift**
When an AI starts generating off-topic or irrelevant material, often due to unclear prompts or outdated context.

**Compression Token Limit**
The maximum number of tokens (words/pieces) an AI model can process in a single prompt and output cycle.

## 🔤 D–F

**DDLC (Documentation Development Life Cycle)**
The lifecycle for writing and maintaining documentation — plan, draft, review, publish, update.

**Deterministic Output**
A predictable, repeatable response from AI. The opposite of creative randomness.

**Embeddings**
Mathematical fingerprints of text — used to compare meaning and context across different words or phrases.

**Fine-tuning**
Customizing a pre-trained AI model using a specific dataset to improve accuracy or tone for a niche use case.

**Failback**
A designed safety phrase or process AI uses when it’s unsure or confused. Example: "Sorry, I didn’t get that. Want to try again?"

**Few-shot Prompting**
Giving an AI a few examples in your prompt to help it understand the format or logic you expect.

**Function Calling**
Letting AI trigger backend tools or code by calling specific functions — turning conversation into action.

---

## 🔤 G–I

**Generative AI**
AI that creates new content — text, images, audio, code — from scratch, based on patterns learned from large datasets.

**Guardrails**
Safety measures that prevent AI misuse — filters, policies, monitoring tools, or design rules that ensure reliability and ethics.

**Grounding (in RAG)**
Tying AI output to a real source or dataset (e.g., your company’s docs) so answers are accurate and verifiable.

**Hallucination**
(See: AI Hallucination)

**Intent (User Intent)**
What the user *really* means or wants to achieve, even if their words are vague.

**Iteration**
The practice of improving AI outputs by tweaking prompts, reviewing results, and repeating until useful.

**Inference**
The process where the AI model takes your input (prompt) and returns a generated response.

**Instruction Tuning**
Training an AI to follow instructions more closely — helpful for building assistant-style behavior.

---

## 🔤 J–L

**Latency**
The time it takes for an AI to respond after a prompt is sent. Lower = better experience.

**LLM (Large Language Model)**
An AI trained on massive amounts of text — able to understand, predict, and generate human-like language.

**Logging**
Keeping records of AI use for review, auditing, and debugging. Often tied to governance and compliance.

**Language Drift**
When the AI gradually changes tone or purpose mid-response — often caused by long context chains or weak constraints.

---

## 🔤 M–O

**Model Architecture**
The structure of how an AI model is built — e.g., transformer-based, encoder-decoder, decoder-only.

**Modular Prompting**
Designing prompts in repeatable building blocks. Makes complex tasks easier to control, reuse, and maintain.

**Multimodal Model**
A model that can understand and combine text, image, audio, and more. Example: GPT-4o or Gemini 1.5 Pro.

**Memory (AI)**
The ability of AI to recall user preferences, past interactions, or long-term context (if enabled).

**Onboarding (AI)**
Helping users understand and adopt AI tools via step-by-step guidance, safe prompts, and value-first demos.

**Output Tokens**
The pieces of text the AI generates. More tokens = longer responses (and potentially higher cost).

---

## 🔤 P–R

**Prompt Engineering**
Crafting clear, strategic prompts to get accurate and aligned outputs from AI. Involves tone, structure, examples.

**Prompt Register**
The tone or voice used in a prompt — formal, playful, critical, etc. Impacts how AI mirrors your request.

**Prompt Pattern**
A template or repeatable structure used to design effective AI prompts. Example: Goal → Constraints → Output Format.

**Re-prompting**
Rewriting or retrying a prompt when the first output doesn’t match expectations.

**RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)**
An architecture that lets AI pull in real data from search or your files before generating an answer.

**Red Teaming (AI)**
Stress-testing AI by trying to break it — surfacing biases, loopholes, safety failures, and misbehavior.

**Response Latency**
How long the AI takes to respond after a question is asked. Can vary based on server load or model type.

**Responsible AI**
The practice of using AI with awareness of ethics, transparency, bias mitigation, and human impact.

---

## 🔤 S–U

**SDLC (Software Development Life Cycle)**
The process followed to build software — from idea to code to testing to release.

**System Confirmation**
When AI repeats or restates your request to ensure it understood correctly before executing.

**Style Guide (AI)**
A documented set of rules for tone, format, terminology, and language style — so your AI stays consistent.

**System Prompt**
A hidden instruction that sets behavior rules for the AI behind the scenes. Example: "You are a helpful assistant."

**Temperature (AI)**
Controls how random or creative the AI is. Lower = focused and factual; higher = creative and risky.

**Token**
The smallest unit of text AI understands — usually 3–4 characters or \~0.75 words.

**Use Case**
A specific task or scenario where AI is applied — like summarizing emails, answering support tickets, or translating policy.

---

## 🔤 V–Z

**Vector Store**
A database of embeddings (see above), often used in RAG systems to help AI retrieve relevant data.

**Voice Prompt**
An instruction spoken out loud to an AI system, often requiring clearer structure and tone design.

**Voiceflow**
A no-code tool used to build conversational AI flows — especially for voice and chat applications.

**Whisper**
OpenAI’s automatic speech recognition model — used to transcribe audio into text.

**Workflow (AI)**
A multi-step process that includes AI at one or more stages — e.g., human → prompt → AI → review → output.

**Zero-shot Prompting**
Asking AI to perform a task without giving it any examples — relies purely on instructions.

## 🏷️ Enterprise Terms Demystified

**LLMOps**
Managing and deploying language models across teams or systems. Like DevOps, but for AI.

**Compliance Risk**
When AI output might violate legal, data privacy, or industry regulations.

**Audit Trail**
A record of what prompts were used, by whom, when — useful for transparency and legal backup.

**Access Control**
Permissions that define who can use AI tools, features, or data within an organization.

**PII (Personally Identifiable Information)**
Sensitive user info (name, email, phone, etc.) that must be protected when using AI responsibly.

**Change Management**
The process of helping people adapt to new systems — like introducing AI without overwhelming your team.

## 🛠️ Prompting & UX Phrases

**Flavor + Function Style**
Our house style: clear, grounded in real use cases, and lightly poetic. Combines utility with tone.

**Human Opener**
A friendly way to begin an interaction with AI — reduces tension and improves trust.

**Confirmation Step**
A question or paraphrase that checks if the AI understood correctly before continuing.

**Turn-based Flow**
Designing AI prompts that feel like a dialogue, not a form — input, reply, clarify, continue.

**Clarifying Prompt**
A follow-up question used to refine ambiguous input. Example: "When you say 'summary', do you mean bullets or a paragraph?"

**Tone Register**
The emotional setting of AI’s response. Examples: confident, curious, empathetic, skeptical.

**Ambient AI**
AI systems that operate in the background, responding to context passively — e.g., voice assistants.
