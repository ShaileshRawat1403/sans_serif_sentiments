## 🧭 Why AI Training Needs Structure

> Untrained users don’t fear AI — they misuse it.

Companies often:

* Announce AI tools without context
* Assume digital-savvy = AI-ready
* Overload teams with tech before strategy

Outcome? Low trust. High misuse. Zero measurable impact.

**Training isn’t just about how to use AI.** It’s about:

* What to use it for
* When not to use it
* What ‘good output’ looks like
* Where your organization draws the line

> ⚠️ AI literacy is now as fundamental as digital literacy was in the 2000s. Without a playbook, teams play roulette.

***

## ❓ What AI Training Is (and Isn’t)

✅ It **is**:

* Role-specific and scenario-based
* Focused on **decision-making**, not button-pushing
* Updated as tools and models evolve
* Integrated with internal comms and workflows

❌ It **isn’t**:

* A one-time workshop
* Tool-centered tutorials with no context
* Generic “how to prompt ChatGPT” videos
* A compliance checkbox

> 🎯 AI training is culture change in disguise. The best programs train habits, not hacks.

## 🧠 The 5 Layers of AI Literacy

| Layer | What It Teaches                             | Audience Level         |
| ----- | ------------------------------------------- | ---------------------- |
| 1     | Basic prompting and interface use           | All employees          |
| 2     | Understanding model strengths/limits        | Power users            |
| 3     | Bias, hallucination, traceability awareness | Compliance, risk teams |
| 4     | Prompt design + prompt testing              | Analysts, comms, L\&D  |
| 5     | Prompt engineering + tool evaluation        | Technical builders     |

> AI literacy is **stacked**, not siloed. Each level enhances the previous, not replaces it.

## 🧍‍♂️ Common Team Personas and Their Struggles

| Persona             | Fear / Misuse                           | What They Need                   |
| ------------------- | --------------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| The Over-Prompter   | Uses 200-word mega-prompts              | Structure + templates            |
| The Copy-Paster     | Blindly pastes output into emails/docs  | Review checklists                |
| The Doubter         | Thinks AI is always wrong or misleading | Bias education, sandbox demos    |
| The Over-Truster    | Believes AI is always right             | Hallucination awareness training |
| The Lurker          | Watches but never tries                 | Low-stakes practice environment  |
| The Shortcut Seeker | Skips prompt reasoning, expects magic   | Context-setting modules          |

> 📌 Train the behavior behind the prompt. Bad mental models create bad output — no matter how good the interface is.

## 🧰 Designing AI Onboarding Programs

**Structure:**

1. **Pre-training survey** → Assess comfort, expectations
2. **Orientation** → What AI is, isn’t, and where it fits
3. **Live walkthrough** → Use a real task + reflect together
4. **Hands-on time** → Practice with role-specific prompts
5. **Output review workshop** → Learn what makes a good response
6. **Resource kit** → Prompts, cheat sheets, feedback forms
7. **Post-training survey** → What changed, what’s unclear

> 🪜 Bonus Layer: Make team leads responsible for prompting fluency in 1:1s and OKRs.

**Program Extensions:**

* Office hours with prompt experts
* Slack channel for feedback/bugs
* Monthly prompt showcase (best, weirdest, most helpful)

## 🧾 What to Include in Training Modules

### ✅ MUST-HAVES

* What is a model vs a chatbot
* What data the model “knows” (and doesn’t)
* How to structure prompts (context → task → format)
* How to rate responses
* What to report (bias, hallucination, stuck loop)
* Where your org prohibits usage (e.g., PII, legal summary)

### ⚠️ NICE-TO-HAVES

* Voice prompt best practices
* Model comparison charts
* API vs interface usage
* Common red flag words to avoid (e.g., "always," "guarantee")

### 🎯 FORMAT SUGGESTIONS

* Microlearning cards (Notion, LMS)
* Scenario quizzes
* Short videos with before/after examples
* Postmortem debrief sessions using AI output

## 🧮 Good, Bad, and Ugly: Real Examples

### 🟢 GOOD: Marketing Team Prompt for Tone Tweaks

> Prompt: “Reword this LinkedIn post to be more empathetic, while keeping the CTA clear.”

**Why it works:**

* Clear task
* Specific instruction
* Useful, editable output

***

### 🟡 BAD: Legal User Copying Entire Contract

> Prompt: “Summarize this contract.” (Pastes 15 pages of legal text)

**Why it’s bad:**

* No scope or instructions
* No tone or risk thresholds
* High risk of missed nuance

***

### 🔴 UGLY: HR User Asking for Interview Feedback

> Prompt: “Give feedback on this candidate's answers.”

**What went wrong:**

* Legal + ethical risks
* No rubric or rating guidance
* Biased or unverifiable suggestions

> 🧯 Ugly usage isn’t always about the result. It’s about the risk exposure.

## 🧠 Reflection Activity: Build a Role-Based AI Guide

Choose a department (e.g., Support, Finance, L\&D).

For each role:

* What’s one task AI could help with?
* What’s the risk if AI gets it wrong?
* How should the prompt be structured?
* Who should review the output?
* What should be logged?
* How often should prompts be revisited or improved?

Then:

* Write 3 sample prompts for that role
* Create a rating checklist for team review
* Share and revise after peer testing
* Create a short video walkthrough for onboarding use

> 🎓 Treat your AI training like a living curriculum. Update it when the tools update.
