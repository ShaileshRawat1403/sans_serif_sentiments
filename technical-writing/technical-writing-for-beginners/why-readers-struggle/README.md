# Why Readers Struggle

_A psychological deep dive into what breaks documentation — and how to fix it._

***

## 🧠 Introduction: The Perspective Trap

> “You know that feeling when you're stuck, frustrated, and hunting for a doc that makes sense?\
> Now imagine you're the one who wrote it — and you're the reason someone else feels that way.”

We’ve all been there. You’re trying to set something up, follow a process, or configure a tool — and the documentation feels like a riddle wrapped in jargon.

You don’t rage. You don’t complain. You just give up or guess your way through.

And then, when you sit down to write docs for someone else — you unconsciously repeat the same problem.\
Why?

Because **writing from someone else’s perspective is a cognitive leap.**\
One that most people don’t even know they need to make.

***

## 🔍 The Science Behind Reader Struggle

### 🧩 1. The Curse of Knowledge

> Once you know something, it’s hard to imagine not knowing it.

This bias is well-documented in cognitive psychology. When we become familiar with a system, we unconsciously assume others share that context.

🔬 _Study:_ A Stanford experiment showed that people who tapped out songs (like “Happy Birthday”) thought listeners would guess them 50% of the time. The actual success rate? 2.5%.

➡ **Docs written from memory of building the product = Docs no one understands.**

***

### 🧠 2. Cognitive Load Theory

The brain can only process a few new things at once. Overloading readers with:

* Too many concepts at once
* Walls of text
* Long unbroken instructions

…leads to **decision fatigue and comprehension failure**.

🧠 _Design takeaway:_ Chunk information. Group related ideas. Give people time to breathe.

***

### 🔎 3. Scanning > Reading

Most users **don’t read**. They scan.

📊 _Nielsen Norman Group_ found that people read online content in an **F-shaped pattern**:

* First few lines fully
* Then just headings and bullet starts
* Then fast-scroll for bold or code blocks

➡ Docs must be **designed** to be scanned. Not read like a novel.

***

## 🎯 Real-World Problem: We Write for Builders, Not Beginners

Here’s what often happens:

| Writer Thought                             | Reader Experience                           |
| ------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------- |
| “They’ll know what that means”             | They Google the term and leave              |
| “It’s intuitive in the UI”                 | They don’t even find the screen             |
| “I followed these steps while building it” | They didn’t build it — they use it          |
| “They can skip to the section they need”   | They don’t even know what section they need |
| “We documented it already”                 | You did. In 3 separate, unlinked pages      |

***

## 💥 The 10 Frustration Triggers

| Frustration                           | Root Cause                | What It Feels Like                     |
| ------------------------------------- | ------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| “Where do I start?”                   | No scope or prerequisites | Being dropped mid-maze                 |
| “What does this even mean?”           | Jargon                    | Translation fatigue                    |
| “This isn’t what I see in the UI”     | Label mismatch            | Mistrust in the doc                    |
| “I did what it said. It didn’t work.” | Skipped dependencies      | Wasted effort                          |
| “Why this step?”                      | No purpose/context        | Blind obedience                        |
| “Do I have permission for this?”      | No role mention           | Hesitation or backtracking             |
| “Can I undo this?”                    | No risk clarification     | Anxiety and fear of breaking something |
| “Where do I go now?”                  | No ‘what’s next’          | Dead end                               |
| “Why 3 docs say different things?”    | No canonical source       | Confusion spiral                       |
| “This sounds like a sales pitch”      | Marketing tone            | Reader feels misled or dismissed       |

## 👤 But You Used a Persona Already: Why Revisit This?

Because writing for a persona (like Priya) isn’t just a UX exercise.\
It’s an _intentional reset of your mental model_.

It helps you switch from:

> “What do I know?” → to → “What will they struggle with?”

Use personas to:

* Focus your word choice
* Design your structure
* Decide what you _don’t_ need to include

🧠 **Tool Tip:**\
Create living personas with [Userforge](https://userforge.com/) or even simple sticky notes.\
Give them goals, fears, and scenarios.

***

## ✅ Reader-Centric Doc Checklist

Use this list as a final gate before publishing your doc:

* [ ] Does this doc explain the _why_, not just the _how_?
* [ ] Have I defined roles, permissions, and prerequisites?
* [ ] Are UI terms **exactly** matched?
* [ ] Did I avoid or define jargon?
* [ ] Is every step logically sequenced?
* [ ] Do I state what happens if something goes wrong?
* [ ] Is the doc scannable (headings, bullets, short steps)?
* [ ] Did I reduce cognitive load with chunked sections?
* [ ] Have I included where to go next?
* [ ] Would this make sense to someone who didn’t build the product?

🧪 **Bonus Test**:\
Watch someone read it.\
Don’t explain. Don’t help.\
Just observe where they stop, squint, or ask “Wait…”

## 🧭 Final Reflection

> If a reader fails to complete the task, it’s not their failure.\
> It’s ours.

Docs are not just instructions.\
They’re **trust contracts** — between a product and its people.

Write like they’re confused.\
Design like they’re tired.\
Test like they’re human.

That’s how you make a doc that doesn’t just deliver — it _lands_.
